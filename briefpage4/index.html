The Land and Its Landscape

Iran is a vast country in West Asia, sharing borders with Iraq to the west, Turkey and Armenia to the northwest, Azerbaijan and Turkmenistan to the north, Afghanistan and Pakistan to the east, and the Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman to the south. Its total land area of approximately 1.648 million square kilometres makes it the 17th-largest country in the world.

The country's terrain is dominated by the Iranian Plateau, one of the great plateaux of Asia. Two major mountain chains frame this interior plateau: the Alborz range in the north and the Zagros range running northwest to southeast. The Alborz reaches its pinnacle at Mount Damavand — at 5,610 metres, the highest peak in the Middle East and the highest volcano in Asia.

To the east and southeast, the plateau descends into two great salt deserts: the Dasht-e Kavir and the Dasht-e Lut. The latter holds the record for the highest surface temperature ever recorded on Earth. Along the northern edge, the Caspian Sea coast features lush subtropical forests, while the southern coast along the Persian Gulf experiences extreme heat and humidity.

Seven Millennia of History

Iran's history stretches back over 7,000 years, making it one of the world's oldest continuous civilisations. The Elamite civilisation emerged around 3200 BCE in what is now southwestern Iran, contemporary with ancient Sumer in Mesopotamia.

The Achaemenid Empire (550–330 BCE), founded by Cyrus the Great, became the largest empire the world had yet seen, stretching from the Balkans to the Indus Valley. Cyrus is remembered for his Cylinder of Human Rights, often considered the first charter of human rights. The empire's capital, Persepolis, remains one of archaeology's most magnificent sites.

After Alexander's conquest, the Parthian and Sassanid empires restored Iranian rule. The Sassanid period (224–651 CE) marked a golden age of Persian art, architecture, and Zoroastrianism. The Arab Muslim conquest in 651 CE brought Islam to Persia, beginning a new chapter in Iranian history.

Culture & Civilisation

Persian culture represents one of humanity's richest artistic and intellectual traditions. The Persian language has produced some of the world's greatest poetry, with figures like Rumi, Hafez, Ferdowsi, and Omar Khayyam achieving universal recognition.

Persian miniature painting, calligraphy, carpet weaving, and architecture are celebrated worldwide. The intricate geometric patterns of Islamic art found their most sophisticated expression in Persian mosques and palaces. The Shah Mosque in Isfahan and the Nasir al-Mulk Mosque in Shiraz exemplify this architectural magnificence.

Nowruz, the Persian New Year, is celebrated on the spring equinox and has been observed for over 3,000 years. It is recognised by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. The celebration reflects Iran's Zoroastrian heritage and the importance of renewal and rebirth in Persian culture.

Economy & Resources

Iran possesses the world's fourth-largest proven oil reserves and second-largest natural gas reserves. The petroleum industry dominates the economy, accounting for a significant portion of government revenue and export earnings.

Beyond hydrocarbons, Iran has substantial mineral resources including copper, iron ore, zinc, and gold. The agricultural sector employs about 18% of the workforce, producing pistachios, saffron, dates, and caviar — Iran is the world's largest producer of saffron and pistachios.

Despite international sanctions, Iran has developed a diversified industrial base including automotive manufacturing, petrochemicals, steel production, and pharmaceuticals. The country has a large educated workforce and significant scientific capabilities.

Persian Language

Persian, known natively as Farsi (فارسی) in Iran, Dari (دری) in Afghanistan, and Tajik (Тоҷикӣ) in Tajikistan, is a member of the Iranian branch of the Indo-European language family. It is one of the world's oldest continuously spoken and written languages, with a documented literary history stretching back over a thousand years.

Persian is written right-to-left using a 32-letter alphabet derived from the Arabic script, with four additional letters added to represent sounds unique to Persian: پ (p), چ (ch), ژ (zh), and گ (g). The script is cursive by nature, giving written Persian a beautiful flowing aesthetic that has been elevated into the major art form of calligraphy.

Persian literature is dominated by poetry to a degree unmatched in almost any other language. For over a thousand years, the ghazal, the masnavi, the rubaʿi, and the qasida have been the primary vehicles for expressing philosophy, love, grief, mysticism, and beauty.
