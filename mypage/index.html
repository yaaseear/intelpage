01 — Origins

What Is the Persian Language?

Persian, known natively as Farsi (فارسی) in Iran, Dari (دری) in Afghanistan, and Tajik (Тоҷикӣ) in Tajikistan, is a member of the Iranian branch of the Indo-European language family. It is one of the world's oldest continuously spoken and written languages, with a documented literary history stretching back over a thousand years and linguistic roots going back three millennia.

Modern Persian evolved from Middle Persian (Pahlavi), the language of the Sassanid Empire (224–651 CE), which itself descended from Old Persian, the language of the Achaemenid kings including Cyrus the Great and Darius I. Old Persian inscriptions at Persepolis and Behistun date to the 6th–5th centuries BCE.

After the Arab conquest of Persia in the 7th century CE, Persian absorbed a massive influx of Arabic vocabulary — particularly in the domains of religion, science, and philosophy — but retained its grammar, syntax, and structural identity entirely. This gives Modern Persian a unique character: grammatically Iranian, lexically enriched by Arabic, and written in a modified Arabic script called the Perso-Arabic script.

02 — Script & Writing

The Persian Script

Persian is written right-to-left using a 32-letter alphabet derived from the Arabic script, with four additional letters added to represent sounds unique to Persian: پ (p), چ (ch), ژ (zh), and گ (g). Unlike Arabic, Persian has no short vowels written in normal text — they are implied by context, much like Hebrew.

The script is cursive by nature: letters connect to each other within words, with some letters having different forms depending on whether they appear at the beginning, middle, or end of a word. This gives written Persian a beautiful flowing aesthetic that has been elevated into a major art form — calligraphy — considered one of the highest arts in Iranian culture.

03 — Literature

A Thousand Years of Poetry

Persian literature is dominated by poetry to a degree unmatched in almost any other language. For over a thousand years, the ghazal, the masnavi, the rubaʿi, and the qasida have been the primary vehicles for expressing philosophy, love, grief, mysticism, and beauty. The Persian poet held — and still holds — a position of enormous cultural prestige.

من کجا بودم عجب کو بود عقلم دوش مست Man kojā budam ajab ku bud aqlam dush mast — "Where was I, how strange — where was my mind last night, drunk" — Rumi

The Great Poets

Ferdowsi (940–1020 CE) composed the Shahnameh — the Book of Kings — a 50,000-couplet epic recounting the mythological and historical past of Persia. Written over thirty years, it is the longest poem ever composed by a single poet, and is credited with single-handedly preserving the Persian language from being replaced by Arabic. Iranians revere Ferdowsi as the saviour of their language.

Omar Khayyam (1048–1131) was simultaneously a brilliant mathematician, astronomer, and the author of the Rubaiyat — four-line verses of elegant skepticism and sensual philosophy. His verses, translated by Edward FitzGerald in 1859, became a worldwide sensation. Khayyam also reformed the Persian solar calendar, producing a system more accurate than the Gregorian calendar introduced five centuries later.

Rumi (Jalal al-Din Muhammad Balkhi, 1207–1273) is arguably the most widely read poet in the world today. Born in Balkh (modern Afghanistan), he settled in Konya (Turkey), where he wrote the six-volume Masnavi — a spiritual epic of 25,000 couplets exploring love, longing, and union with the divine. His Divan-e Shams-e Tabrizi contains over 40,000 verses of ecstatic poetry.

Hafez (Khwaja Shams-ud-Din Muhammad Hafez-e Shirazi, 1315–1390) is considered the master of the ghazal and the pinnacle of Persian lyric poetry. His Divan is the most widely-owned book in Iran after the Quran, and is used as an oracle — a practice called Fal-e Hafez, where one opens the book at random to receive guidance.

عشق آتش است و من خرمن سوخته Eshq atash ast va man kharman sukhte — "Love is fire, and I am a burnt harvest" — Hafez
04 — Influence

Persian's Influence on World Languages

For several centuries during the medieval period, Persian served as the lingua franca of scholarship, diplomacy, and culture across a vast stretch of the Islamic world — from the Ottoman Empire in the west to the Mughal Empire in the east. Thousands of Persian words entered Turkish, Arabic, Urdu, Hindi, Bengali, and even English through trade and conquest.

English words of Persian origin include: azure (from lazaward), candy (from qand), chess (from chatrang/shatranj), caravan (kārvan), jasmine (yāsamin), lemon (līmū), orange (nārang), paradise (pairidaēza — meaning "walled garden"), shawl (shāl), scarlet (saqerlāt), spinach (aspanāx), and taffeta (tāfteh). The word "magic" comes from the Old Persian maguš — a Zoroastrian priest.

The Mughal Empire in India (1526–1858) used Persian as its official court language. All imperial decrees, poetry, and official correspondence were in Persian. The Taj Mahal's inscriptions are in Persian. This introduced enormous amounts of Persian vocabulary into Hindi and Urdu — creating what linguists call Indo-Persian cultural synthesis.

05 — History of the Language

Three Stages of Persian

600–300 BCE — OLD PERSIAN

Spoken in the Achaemenid Empire. Inscribed in cuneiform on rock faces at Behistun and Persepolis. Used by Darius I and Xerxes. Closely related to Avestan — the language of Zoroastrianism's sacred texts.

200 BCE–800 CE — MIDDLE PERSIAN (PAHLAVI)

Language of the Parthian and Sassanid empires. Written in its own script. Rich literary tradition of religious and secular texts. The Bundahishn, Denkard, and Arda Wiraz Namag are major surviving works.

800 CE–PRESENT — NEW PERSIAN

Emerges after the Arab conquest, written in modified Arabic script with Arabic loanwords. Remarkably stable — a speaker of 10th-century Persian can largely read modern texts. The first major New Persian literary work is the Shahnameh (1010 CE).

1979–PRESENT — MODERN STANDARDISATION

After the Islamic Revolution, Iran's Academy of Persian Language and Literature began systematically creating Persian equivalents for Western technical terms to reduce foreign word borrowing. "Telescope" became durbin, "computer" became rāyāneh.

06 — Grammar

Key Grammar Features

Persian grammar is notably simpler than many European languages in some respects. There is no grammatical gender — the same word and pronoun (او, "u") refers to both he and she. Nouns do not decline for case. Adjectives follow nouns and are connected by the ezāfe particle (-e or -ye), creating noun phrases of great elegance: for example, "ketāb-e Rumi" means "the book of Rumi" or "Rumi's book."

Verbs are richly conjugated and placed at the end of sentences, giving Persian its Subject-Object-Verb word order. The verb "to be" (budan) has both present and past forms, and Persian uses a unique compound past tense formed by combining the past participle with the present tense of "to be" — a feature it shares with French and German. The subjunctive mood is alive and extensively used in everyday speech, unlike in most modern European languages.

Persian distinguishes formal and informal registers extensively. The pronoun shomā (شما) is the formal "you" used with elders, strangers, and in professional settings, while to (تو) is informal. This distinction permeates not just pronouns but verb conjugations, titles, and the entire vocabulary of politeness — a system called ta'arof that is central to Iranian social interaction.
