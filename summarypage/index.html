01 — Geography

The Land and Its Landscape

Iran is a vast country in West Asia, sharing borders with Iraq to the west, Turkey and Armenia to the northwest, Azerbaijan and Turkmenistan to the north, Afghanistan and Pakistan to the east, and the Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman to the south. Its total land area of approximately 1.648 million square kilometres makes it the 17th-largest country in the world.

The country's terrain is dominated by the Iranian Plateau, one of the great plateaux of Asia. Two major mountain chains frame this interior plateau: the Alborz range in the north and the Zagros range running northwest to southeast. The Alborz reaches its pinnacle at Mount Damavand — at 5,610 metres, the highest peak in the Middle East and the highest volcano in Asia.

To the east and southeast, the plateau descends into two great salt deserts: the Dasht-e Kavir (Great Salt Desert) and the Dasht-e Lut (Desert of Emptiness). The Dasht-e Lut has been recorded as one of the hottest surfaces on Earth, with land-surface temperatures measured at over 70°C by satellites in 2005 and 2006.

Iran's rivers are relatively few and generally modest in flow. The Karun, flowing through Khuzestan province, is the only navigable river. The Zayandeh River irrigates the ancient city of Isfahan. The Lake Urmia in the northwest — once the largest lake in the Middle East — has severely shrunk in recent decades due to drought, diversion, and mismanagement.

Iran's climate varies enormously. The Caspian coast in the north is subtropical and humid — lush green forests receive up to 2,000 mm of rainfall annually. The central plateau is arid and semi-arid, with temperature extremes. The northern plains are fertile, the western highlands are cold and snowy in winter, and the southern coast is hot and humid year-round.

02 — History

Seven Thousand Years of Civilisation

The territory of modern Iran has been inhabited for at least 7,000 years. Some of the earliest known farming communities arose on the Iranian plateau. The Elamite civilisation, centred on Susa in today's Khuzestan, emerged around 3200 BCE and developed one of the earliest writing systems alongside ancient Sumer.

The Persian Empires

The Medes, an Iranian people, unified much of the plateau into the first Iranian empire around 678 BCE. The Achaemenid Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great in 550 BCE, became the largest empire the world had ever seen — stretching from the Balkans and Egypt to Central Asia and northwestern India. Cyrus is remarkable in world history for his Cylinder, considered one of the first declarations of human rights, freeing the Babylonian Jews from captivity.

Darius I expanded the empire further and created a sophisticated administrative system with provinces (satrapies), a road network including the Royal Road, and a standardised currency. The empire thrived until Alexander the Great conquered it between 334–330 BCE, though Persian culture and language quickly asserted themselves over the Macedonian conquerors.

The Sassanid Empire (224–651 CE) represented a cultural and architectural renaissance. Grand palaces, intricate silverwork, and the spread of Zoroastrianism — the world's first monotheistic religion — defined this era. The Arab conquest introduced Islam to the Persians, who would, over centuries, transform it through their own philosophical traditions, giving rise to a uniquely Persian-Islamic synthesis in art, architecture, and mystical thought known as Sufism.

The Safavid dynasty (1501–1736) brought political unity and architectural brilliance. Isfahan under Shah Abbas I became one of the most beautiful cities in the world, and the declaration of Twelver Shi'a Islam as the state religion created Iran's enduring identity as the spiritual heart of Shia Islam.

03 — Culture & Society

The Persian Mind — Literature, Art, and Tradition

Persian culture is one of the oldest continuous civilisations on Earth. The Persian language — Farsi — has been spoken and written for over three thousand years. It was the language of diplomacy, literature, and scholarship across a vast region from Istanbul to Delhi during the medieval era.

Poetry: The Soul of Iran

Persian literature is dominated by its poetry tradition. The Shahnameh (Book of Kings) by Ferdowsi, completed in 1010 CE, is a fifty-thousand-couplet epic recounting the mythical and historical past of Persia — the longest poem written by a single author in world literature. It is credited with preserving the Persian language through centuries of Arabic dominance.

Omar Khayyam (1048–1131) was a mathematician, astronomer, and poet whose Rubaiyat became one of the most widely translated works in the world. Rumi (1207–1273), born in Khorasan, wrote the Masnavi — six volumes of spiritual poetry exploring love, loss, and the soul's longing for the divine. Hafez (1315–1390), writing in Shiraz, is considered the master of the ghazal form. His Divan is found in nearly every Iranian home today and is used for divination.

"Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field. I'll meet you there." — Rumi

Architecture

Iranian architecture is a world heritage in itself. The Palace of Persepolis, begun by Darius I around 518 BCE, combined Achaemenid, Egyptian, Greek, and Mesopotamian influences into a monumental ceremonial complex. The Friday Mosque of Isfahan (Masjid-e Jame) is an architectural museum spanning twelve centuries, demonstrating every major style from the Seljuk to the Safavid. The blue-tiled dome of the Imam Mosque in Isfahan is considered one of the most beautiful buildings on Earth.

Nowruz — The Persian New Year

Nowruz, the Persian New Year celebrated on the spring equinox (around March 20), is Iran's most important festival. It predates Islam by thousands of years, rooted in Zoroastrian tradition. Families prepare a symbolic table called the Haft Sin — seven items beginning with the letter S that represent renewal, health, prosperity, patience, love, beauty, and the rising of the sun. In 2010, UNESCO added Nowruz to its Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

04 — Economy

Oil, Sanctions, and Resilience

Iran holds the world's fourth-largest proven oil reserves (approximately 157 billion barrels) and the world's second-largest natural gas reserves (approximately 34 trillion cubic metres). Historically, the oil sector has been the backbone of government revenue, though decades of sanctions have forced diversification.

Iran's economy is a mixed one: the state controls major industries including energy, banking, and heavy manufacturing, while the private sector operates in agriculture, trade, and services. The bazaar — the traditional marketplace — remains a powerful economic and cultural institution. Tehran's Grand Bazaar is one of the largest covered bazaars in the world.

The 1979 revolution and subsequent nationalisation of industries disrupted Western investment, and the Iran-Iraq war of the 1980s devastated the economy. From the 1990s onward, US, EU, and UN sanctions — particularly targeting oil exports, banking, and technology — have constrained economic growth, caused currency depreciation, and fuelled inflation. Despite this, Iran has developed a diversified industrial base including automobiles, chemicals, steel, and pharmaceuticals largely through domestic production.

Agriculture and Beyond

Iran is one of the world's largest producers of pistachios, saffron, and dried fruits — all significant export earners. Saffron, the world's most expensive spice by weight, is cultivated in the Khorasan region. Iran accounts for roughly 90% of global saffron production. The country also produces significant quantities of wheat, rice, barley, dates, and citrus fruits.

05 — Politics & Government

The Islamic Republic — A Unique Political Structure

Iran's government is a constitutional theocracy — a unique hybrid of republican and Islamic institutions. The Supreme Leader (Rahbar) is the highest authority in the land, responsible for general policy, command of the armed forces, and oversight of all branches. The current Supreme Leader is Ali Khamenei, holding office since 1989 following the death of the founder, Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini.

An elected President leads the executive branch and oversees the day-to-day administration of government. The Islamic Consultative Assembly (Majlis) is the legislature with 290 elected members. All candidates for election are vetted by the Guardian Council — twelve jurists and Islamic scholars who can disqualify candidates deemed insufficiently loyal to Islamic principles.

Iran has a complicated and often adversarial relationship with the United States, rooted in the 1953 CIA-backed coup that ousted the democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh, and the 1979 hostage crisis. Relations with Israel are also deeply hostile; Iran does not recognise the Israeli state. The nuclear programme remains a central issue of global concern, with Iran asserting the right to civilian nuclear power while Western powers fear weapons development.

06 — Science & Intellectual Legacy

The House of Wisdom and Its Heirs

Iran's intellectual tradition stretches back millennia. During the Islamic Golden Age (8th–14th centuries), Persian scholars made foundational contributions to mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and philosophy. Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi, born in the Khwarazm region, gave algebra its name (from his book Al-Kitab al-mukhtasar fi hisab al-jabr) and his Latinised name gave us the word "algorithm."

Avicenna (Ibn Sina, 980–1037), born near Bukhara, wrote the Canon of Medicine — a medical encyclopaedia that remained the standard textbook in European medical schools until the 17th century. Omar Khayyam derived a geometric solution to cubic equations and reformed the Persian calendar with an accuracy exceeding the Gregorian calendar. Nasir al-Din al-Tusi built the Maragheh observatory and wrote foundational works in trigonometry.

Today, Iran's scientific output has grown significantly despite sanctions. It ranks among the top producers of scientific papers in the region, particularly in nanoscience, polymer chemistry, and materials science. Iran's universities graduate large numbers of engineers and scientists annually. The country launched its first domestic satellite, Omid, in 2009.

This page is built with three core web technologies: HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Below are the key techniques used — each explained with a working example.

01 — HTML Semantic Structure

HTML (HyperText Markup Language) defines the content and structure of a page using nested elements called "tags". Semantic tags like <section>, <header>, and <nav> describe meaning, not just appearance.

Basic Example
<section id="geography"> <h2>The Land</h2> <p>Iran spans 1.64M km²...</p> </section> /* Each section = one topic block */ /* id="" lets CSS and JS target it */

02 — CSS Custom Properties (Variables)

CSS variables (defined with --) let you store reusable values like colours and fonts. Change one variable and every element using it updates automatically — the backbone of any design system.

Basic Example
:root { --gold: #c9a84c; --bg: #0a0a0a; } h2 { /* uses the variable */ color: var(--gold); } body { background: var(--bg); }

03 — CSS @keyframes Animations

The summary bar and hero text use @keyframes — a CSS feature that defines an animation's start and end states. The "animation-delay" property staggers each line, creating the fast sequential load effect you see at the top.

Basic Example
/* Define the animation */ @keyframes slideDown { from { transform: translateY(-100%); } to { transform: translateY(0); } } /* Apply it to the bar */ #summary-bar { animation: slideDown 0.4s ease 0.1s forwards; } /* Stagger each line */ .sum-line:nth-child(2) { animation-delay: 0.68s; }

04 — CSS Grid Layout

CSS Grid creates two-dimensional layouts. The data cards (capitals, populations etc.) use a grid that automatically fills columns. "auto-fill" with "minmax" makes it responsive: cards rearrange on small screens without any JavaScript.

Basic Example
.data-grid { display: grid; /* Fill as many 200px cols as fit */ grid-template-columns: repeat(auto-fill, minmax(200px, 1fr)); /* 1px gap = thin border effect */ gap: 1px; background: var(--border); }

05 — CSS position: fixed

The summary bar stays visible as you scroll because of "position: fixed". This removes the element from the normal document flow and pins it relative to the viewport. "top: 0" and "z-index: 1000" keep it glued to the top, above all other content.

Basic Example
#summary-bar { /* Pins to viewport, not page */ position: fixed; top: 0; /* glued to top */ left: 0; right: 0; /* full width */ /* Renders above other elements */ z-index: 1000; }

06 — CSS ::before Pseudo-Elements

"::before" inserts decorative content before an element without adding HTML. The timeline dots and the hero background glows are entirely CSS — no extra HTML tags needed. This keeps the HTML clean and the design flexible.

Basic Example
.tl-item::before { /* Required to display */ content: ''; /* Position relative to parent */ position: absolute; left: -33px; top: 7px; /* The visible dot */ width: 9px; height: 9px; background: var(--gold); border-radius: 50%; }

07 — Web Fonts via <link>

Typography is loaded from Google Fonts using a <link> tag in the <head>. This fetches font files from Google's CDN. The CSS font-family property then applies them. Three fonts are used here: Playfair Display for titles, IBM Plex Mono for labels, and Lora for body text.

Basic Example
<!-- In the HTML <head> --> <link href="https://fonts.googleapis.com /css2?family=Playfair+Display: wght@700&display=swap" rel="stylesheet"/> /* Then use in CSS */ h1 { font-family: 'Playfair Display', serif; }

08 — CSS clamp() for Fluid Type

The hero title scales smoothly between mobile and desktop using clamp(). It takes three values: minimum, preferred, maximum. Instead of jumping at breakpoints, the font grows proportionally with the viewport width — no JavaScript required.

Basic Example
.hero-title { /* min=64px, ideal=10vw, max=128px */ font-size: clamp(64px, 10vw, 128px); } /* On a 900px screen: 10vw = 90px → used (in range) On a 500px screen: 10vw = 50px → clamped to 64px On a 2000px screen: 10vw = 200px → clamped to 128px */
